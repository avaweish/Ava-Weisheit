Beer Classification Decision Tree

Data analysis, model training, and performance evaluation using sensory beer attributes.

Overview

This project explored how different sensory characteristics of beer—such as bitterness, sweetness, sourness, hoppiness, and maltiness— can be used to predict beer styles using a decision tree classifier. The goal was to determine whether measurable taste features could accurately distinguish beer types and to visualize model performance through a detailed decision tree, confusion matrix, and classification report.

My Role

I was responsible for data preprocessing, model training, and performance evaluation. I split the dataset into 70% training and 30% testing groups, trained multiple decision tree models at various depths, and plotted accuracy scores to identify the optimal model configuration. I selected a max depth of 5 to balance performance and overfitting, then visualized the final tree using plot_tree() in Matplotlib. I also interpreted the decision rules, precision scores, and color-coded node accuracy to explain model behavior.

Key Findings

The decision tree revealed clear patterns in how sensory variables relate to beer classification. Styles like IPA and Sour were highly distinguishable, achieving precision scores of 0.66 and 0.79 respectively, while beers such as Lambic and Porter proved more difficult to classify. The confusion matrix showed strong diagonal values for easily separable styles but significant off-diagonal activity for overlapping ones, suggesting that sensory attributes alone may not capture the full complexity of beer differentiation. The classification report confirmed this, with an overall accuracy of 59% and an average F1-score of 0.57, indicating moderate predictive success and room for improvement with more advanced models or additional features.
